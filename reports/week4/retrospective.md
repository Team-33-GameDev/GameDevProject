## What Went Well

1. **MVP v2 Delivery:** The team successfully delivered the complete core loop, including complex features like the modifier system, death phase teleportation, and original music composition.
2. **Customer Communication:** The team effectively demonstrated the prototype and clearly articulated the technical achievements, leading to a smooth approval process.
3. **Receptiveness to Feedback:** The team quickly grasped the customer's critique regarding the passive clicking mechanic and immediately began brainstorming physical interaction solutions.

## What Did Not Go Well

1. **Design Execution vs. Concept:** While the Vision Document described a high-stakes survival test, the actual implementation resulted in a passive clicking experience. The design pillar of "physical attention" was not fully realized in code.
2. **Testing Preparation:** The team was unprepared for the automated testing requirements of Assignment 4, leading to uncertainty about how to proceed with CI and quality gates.
3. **Visual Identity:** The 3D room model, while functional, lacks a distinct visual identity and relies too heavily on existing market references.

## What Changed Compared to Previous Sprint

- **Focus Shift:** In Sprint 2, the focus was purely on technical implementation of the core loop. In Sprint 3, the focus shifts heavily to game design refinement (mechanic engagement) and visual identity, based directly on customer feedback.
- **Testing Integration:** Unlike previous sprints where testing was manual, Sprint 4 will introduce formal automated testing and CI pipelines to meet Assignment 4 requirements.
